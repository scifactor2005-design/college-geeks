Theory of Computation

Explore the mathematical foundations of computing and the limits of what can be computed.

Introduction

Theory of Computation deals with how efficiently problems can be solved on a model of computation, using an algorithm. It is divided into automata theory, computability theory, and complexity theory.

Finite Automata

Finite Automata are abstract machines used to recognize patterns. They are the simplest model of computation and are used to design lexical analyzers. Types include DFA (Deterministic) and NFA (Non-deterministic).

Context-Free Grammars (CFG)

CFGs are used to describe the syntax of programming languages. They are more powerful than regular expressions and are recognized by Pushdown Automata.

Pushdown Automata

Pushdown Automata are finite automata with a stack memory. They can recognize Context-Free Languages, which include most programming language constructs.

Turing Machines

A Turing Machine is a mathematical model of computation that defines an abstract machine. It is capable of simulating any computer algorithm and is used to study the limits of computation.

Complexity Theory

Complexity Theory classifies problems based on their inherent difficulty. Key classes include P (solvable in polynomial time) and NP (verifiable in polynomial time).
